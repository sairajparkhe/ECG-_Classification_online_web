    ABOUT

    ECG Classification and Arrhythmia Detection serves as our final year project, employing cutting-edge technology to identify irregularities in heart function promptly and mitigate potential complications. By harnessing the latest advancements, this project aims to enhance the timely detection of cardiac abnormalities, thus enabling early intervention and preventing adverse health outcomes. Through the utilization of state-of-the-art methods, such as deep learning algorithms and convolutional neural networks, the system analyzes electrocardiogram (ECG) signals to accurately classify different types of arrhythmias. By doing so, it seeks to provide healthcare professionals with valuable insights for proactive management and personalized treatment strategies. Ultimately, this project endeavors to contribute to the advancement of cardiovascular care by leveraging modern technologies to safeguard heart health and improve patient outcomes.

    FAQs

    What is ECG ?

    ECG stands for Electrocardiogram. It is a medical test that measures the electrical activity of the heart over a period of time. The ECG machine records the impulses that trigger each heartbeat and produces a graphical representation known as an electrocardiogram. During an ECG, small electrodes are attached to the skin on the chest, arms, and legs. These electrodes detect the electrical signals generated by the heart as it contracts and relaxes. The resulting ECG waveform provides valuable information about the heart's rhythm, the conduction system, and any potential abnormalities. Healthcare professionals use ECGs for various purposes, such as diagnosing heart conditions, monitoring the effects of medications, assessing the overall health of the heart, and detecting any signs of cardiac abnormalities or diseases. ECGs are a common and important tool in cardiology and are often part of routine medical examinations and assessments.

    What is Arrhythmia?

    A heart arrhythmia (uh-RITH-me-uh) is an irregular heartbeat. A heart arrhythmia occurs when the electrical signals that tell the heart to beat don't work properly. The heart may beat too fast or too slow. Or the pattern of the heartbeat may be inconsistent. A heart arrhythmia may feel like a fluttering, pounding or racing heartbeat. Some heart arrhythmias are harmless. Others may cause life-threatening symptoms.
